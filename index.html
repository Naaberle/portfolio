Dissonance as Harmony (2025)

This installation was the culmination of a semester long residency at the Art Center of Brazos Valley where I conducted a wide range of research on visual and interactive techniques. A key component of that research was testing out different membranes to be used as part of a system that created Lissajous curves with lasers. Once I had an ideal membrane for the space I was working in I utilized five Time of Flight sensors aligned in a horizontal array to measure the distances of up to five viewers and convert that distance into a frequency. Five people working together could create a complex, resonant pattern and see the Lissajous curve that represented their efforts. The objective of the project was to introduce viewers to not only the technology aspect, but also perceptual art where two or more of your senses might conflict on what is pleasant or grating.

Caste of Education (2024)

Caste of Education was an interactive installation I created for a time-based media class. We were tasked with picking a well known story/fairy tale to use as inspiration and retelling it in a new way. I chose the Cask of Amontillado as inspiration, and created a 4' x 4' x 4' box that viewers could crawl in and close the door, activating the wall of HDMI displays I embedded in the plywood. The story I tell is one of American students being bricked up by student debt and forgotten about while trying to survive in the current economic climate.

The walls of the box, including the cut-outs for the displays, were designed in Adobe Illustrator and Autodesk Fusion and cut out on a Techno CNC Systems TITAN series router.

Destructive Interference (2025)

Destructive Interference is a data visualization installation that representing the deaths and injuries caused by mass shootings in 2024 in the United States. I parametrically designed and fabricated an interlocking ring sculpture for each month of 2024; where the overall height corresponds to the level of violence in that month. Taller forms mark the deadliest months, while shorter ones reflect fewer casualties. Each inner ring encodes the number of people killed or injured, and each outer ring encodes the number of shootings and the number of days without them. The interlocking cylinders are powered via a motor to rotate, and lit from within. As the cylinders rotate, they cast overlapping shadows that represent those killed or injured by mass shootings. The goal of this work is to visualize otherwise overwhelming and disparate statistics in a way that is both physically present and emotionally resonant. By inviting viewers to step into and engage with these shadows, the piece creates space for reflection, conversation, and confrontation with the scale of this ongoing crisis.

Perceptual Surfaces (2025)

Perceptual Surfaces was a series I created while trying to figure out the best way to compensate for the persistence of vision issues present in 3D Hologram Fans. Spinning at 700 RPMS the density of pixels towards the center is far great than towards the outside edge. As a result, attempting to put straight lines on the display resulted in aliasing and curved lines. I started trying to map it, both as a way to work around it and as a way to exploit it. I discovered if you put a standard moire pattern created from 2 sets of 2 pixel lines rotating opposite of each other you can exploit the aliasing to create a moire pattern that is the result of both the digital input and limitations of the mechanical device. Additionally, you can't photograph it properly because digital camera sensors and shutter speed add their own element of moire. As a result I named these "perceptual surfaces" since they take advantage of human perception specifically to be displayed properly and are best viewed in person.

The Promptocene: Gallery (2025)

The Promptocene: Gallery was a purely digital digital twin installation showcasing what a possible AI gallery would look like in the future. Using simulated data to represent real art gallery traffic, viewers are profiled in the digital twin to determine what type of artwork they like the most and then an AI model trained on that art creates a supposedly ideal image for them at the end of the gallery tour. Based on their reaction the model adapts for the next viewer, eventually creating the perfect images for everyone. It takes the subjectivity of art and tries to objectify it, while still be concerned with the subjective taste of the viewer.

Two Unreal environments were created. One for real gallery installation and sensor data that was fed by simulated MQTT data, and one designed to be a VR experience where the VR attendee and headset are being tracked as the sensor input data.

Version Control (2026)

Version Control was designed for an art call with the specific intent of defining an artist's practice. I chose to make an installation that incorporated wiring, electronics, and interactivity in a way that I have in the past but display it as a sketch of an installation. Once the installation was finalized in my head I set out to build it in my garage as a mock up like my previous installations. But instead of taking a refining pass where I clean up wire lines, replace pieces of scrap wood with polished pieces, and cast the final piece for the LED strip I got to the first stage of everything working and then installed that in the gallery. I wanted viewers to see my process and practice while still maintaining all of the requirements for the installations to be considered finished in my eyes.

There are three interactive buttons that feed to a central Raspberry Pi. The top button activates a laser and speaker to create a Lissajous curve on an adjacent wall. The middle button activates a 3D Hologram fan that loops a geometric animation I created in Houdini. And the bottom button triggers a waterfall effect on the LED strip. Mosfet modules between the power rails and end points are controlled by the three buttons to ensure there isn't constant power being sent and the buttons being pressed control when that power is sent.
